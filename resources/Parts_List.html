Hardware for ME405: Preliminary List

It is possible that due to the ongoing pandemic, ME405 may have to shift from in-person to online instruction. The course is being designed to facilitate such a shift should it become necessary. We’re stockpiling as many components for checkout as we can, but if the shift happens, you’ll need tools and components with which to work. The following list contains some recommended hardware for ME405. You are not required to buy the specific components listed, and your instructor is #not_sponsored by any of the vendors; other compatible tools and components are fine, and reusing equipment from a previous course is encouraged.

Measurement Tools

Everyone should purchase at least a multimeter and cheap logic analyzer; you’ll use these in this course, other courses, your career, and probably around the house for many years.

Digital Multimeter

Week 1 Remote Recommended
A cheap multimeter is sufficient for ME405. Even an old analog multimeter is sufficient as long as it has high input impedance (≥10MΩ).

https://www.amazon.com/AstroAI-Digital-Multimeter-Voltage-Tester/dp/B01ISAMUA6
https://www.harborfreight.com/7-Function-Digital-Multimeter-63759.html

Logic Analyzer and/or ’Scope

Week 1 Remote Recommended
An inexpensive USB logic analyzer is very helpful for diagnosing problems with digital communication channels, PWM signals, and other digital signals. There are dozens of brand names for sub-$20 analyzers, and nearly all seem to be essentially identical.

https://www.amazon.com/KeeYees-Analyzer-Device-Channel-Arduino/dp/B07K6HXDH1/

WindowsTM Note: Installing the drivers on this OS can be a pain, though it’s worth it to have the analyzer working. If your system won’t detect the analyzer and you have a Bluetooth mouse, try unplugging the mouse adapter (substitute a USB mouse with a cord) while you install the drivers for the logic analyzer.

If you’re not in financial difficulty, you might consider a USB oscilloscope with logic analyzer such as the following:

https://www.amazon.com/Hantek-Portable-Oscilloscope-Analyzer-Diagnostic/dp/B01N74XEAN

Breadboard

Week 1 Remote Recommended
Any small solderless breadboard with at least 400 contact points should be fine. An example which comes in a set with convenient color-coded wires (the use of color coded wires is highly recommended) is shown below.

Motor Power Supply

The microcontroller board can be powered through its USB connector, but motors need a separate power supply. A generic “wall wart” is usually fine as long as it can produce at least two amps or so at the desired voltage. To use the very cheap driver board below, you need a 9 volt power supply. A supply which comes with an adapter for its plug is extra helpful. Examples:

https://www.amazon.com/Charger-Switching-Adapter-Speakers-Transformer/dp/B08SKY1P8G/
https://www.amazon.com/inShareplus-Voltage-Transformer-100-240V-Connector/dp/B07KVZKWD9

Components

Microcontroller on a board

We’re trying to scrape up enough Nucleo circuit boards and Shoes of Brian to enable one to be checked out to each student. However, as boards are sometimes damaged during class (Burn by Doing) and we don’t have a lot of spares, it may be necessary to find more or make substitutions.

  • STM32L476RG Nucleo. These boards are currently out of stock at major distributors such as Mouser and Digi-Key, but occasionally a few show up. Ordering directly from ST may be an option.

    One can use some other Nucleos, but they don’t have as much flash storage space as the STM32L476RG, which causes problems when you need to use several program files. Boards which can be used in a limited way include the following:
    • STM32F401RE Nucleo
    • STM32F411RE Nucleo
    • STM32F446RE Nucleo

    Examples:
    https://www.mouser.com/ and search for “Nucleo STM32L476”
    https://www.digikey.com/product-detail/en/stmicroelectronics/NUCLEO-L476RG/497-15881-ND/5347711

  • ESP32 prototyping boards may be used for term projects with some additional work to port the software from one processor type to another. ESP32s have WiFi and Bluetooth radios built in. However, the ESP32’s peripherals such as A/D converter, PWM, and counter/timers aren’t as good (in the lab instructor’s opinion) as those of the STM32, and they don’t work with the Shoe of Brian, so you have to set up a different way to connect sensors and motor drivers. AdaFruit Feathers are more expensive and have fewer pins than some other boards but include a built-in LiPo battery management system that allows convenient charging and wireless use.

    Examples: (Week 1 Remote Recommended unless you will be isolating in San Luis Obispo, in which case we may be able to arrange an outdoor, isolated pickup of a ME405 kit with Nucleo and Shoe)

    https://www.digikey.com/en/products/detail/espressif-systems/ESP32-DEVKITM-1/13532113
    https://www.adafruit.com/product/3591

Small DC motor

You’ll need a small DC motor with some sort of position encoder. It is recommended to wait until the term project is defined before choosing a geared motor, as the gear ratio will need to be specified as part of your design.

Note that a motor’s voltage rating is the maximum voltage at which it is designed to run without damage. You do not need to run the motor at exactly that voltage, so for example, a 12V motor can be run from a 9V supply; it won’t produce as much torque, but it will run. You should ensure that the supply voltage is more than half the rated voltage, though, to ensure that the motor isn’t so weak as to hardly move.

https://www.adafruit.com/product/4638
https://www.amazon.com/330RPM-Geared-Encoder-Electric-Reduction/dp/B08DDC8KFK

Assembly Tools

Soldering Iron

We have good soldering stations in the lab, but lab access may be restricted. It is recommended that if you choose to solder, you have soldering gear of your own or at least available to you at your home (but never anywhere near food).

The following irons are some of the least expensive whose quality and safety are reasonably acceptable. There are similar looking imitations for about the same price whose quality is unknown to us. You’ll probably also need some extra tips if you do much soldering; some people prefer the pointed tips linked below, while others prefer flatter “screwdriver” shaped tips (but get the small ones, not large ones which are for soldering water pipes and such). Make sure the tips you buy specifically match the model number of iron, as there are many incompatible tip styles.

https://www.amazon.com/Weller-WLC100-40-Watt-Soldering-Station/dp/B000AS28UC
https://www.amazon.com/Baitaihem-Replacement-Soldering-SP40NUS-SP40NKUS/dp/B07G599QV9

If you’re sure you’ll be doing a lot of mechatronic hardware work in the future, investing in a good soldering iron is recommended. We’ve had good luck with these:

https://www.amazon.com/Hakko-FX888D-23BY-Digital-Soldering-Station/dp/B00ANZRT4M

Pliers, Tweezers, and Tiny Screwdrivers:

Tweezers with sharply pointed tips are very helpful for assembly of small items; most drugstores carry them. Small needle-nosed pliers and screwdrivers are helpful and available at most hardware stores. Small diagonal cutters are also an important part of one’s toolkit.
